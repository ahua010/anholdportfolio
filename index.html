Welcome to my portfolio! My name is Anh.

Movabile

Mobile App Design and Prototype

  • Date: Autumn 2015
  • Skills: Goal-Directed Design, Basalmiq, Adobe Illustrator
  • Category: User Experience Design

Overview

Project created for Introduction to User-Centered Design class (HCDE 318) - Autumn 2015
In this project, I worked in a group of 3, using the Goal-Directed Design process to create a solution to solve a real world problem. The problem that we chose to solve is to help people with mobility impairments navigate the UW campus more easily.
Role: User experience designer, visual designer.

Problem

The problems we wanted to address are the obstacles the mobility impaired population face daily that are not visible in the eyes of the able-bodied population. Without the flexibility that the majority of people have, people with mobility impairments have a more limited set of options to get around. The obstacles, as simple as a bumpy road or as complex as the rain, could stand on the way of achieving a more independent lifestyle and discourage people from maximizing their potentials.

Design Process

We followed the Goal-Directed Design process, and went through all of the steps in 10 weeks. The result was a high-fidelity mock-up and a formal in-class presentation.

Phase 1: Research

Web Research

This is the start of the project. Having decided that we wanted to look into problems that users with mobility impairments faced in their daily life and helped to solve it, we started to build our understanding by conducting web research. This phase is to ensure that our design decisions would be grounded in facts rather than assumptions. After that, I composed a paper together with the group to summarize our finding and sources. This step was used as a way to think about our potential users in a more specific context and how to understand them better to be able to design for their needs.

Research Findings

 

Phase 2: Modeling

In this phase, utilized the information from the first phase, we created user models - detailed archetypes that represents behaviors, goals, and motivations of our targeted users.

Develop Personas and Scenarios

From the information gathered from the research, we started creating personas - people who would benefit from this product. Through our personas we were able to determine what our personas would do and behave in certain scenarios in their daily lives.
The personas we developed represented 2 differents set of user demographics - a college student with a temporary injury, and a professor who was a long-term wheelchair user. Each of them had different needs, pains, and desires regarding mobility when they went about their daily life.

Personas
Scenarios

Sketches

From this point of the project, we had a fairly good understanding of our users and their needs. We then applied divergent thinking and designed different technological solutions to meet their needs. We then went through a critique sessions, debated the feasibility, strength, and originality of the sketches, then picked out the top 3 designs, and thought about ways to improve them.

Phase 3: Framework

After narrowing down on the solution and achieving a clearer vision of our product, we embarked on the process to create the overall product concept, define the framework for the product behavior, and work on the information architecture aspect.

Sitemap

We thought about the flow of information as well as interactions of our product. After going through several revisions, we arrived at the final version, which was created with Illustrator.

Paper Prototype

After we completed our sitemap, we were able to see the series of steps that it would take to complete certain tasks. We hand drew our design on paper to create a prototype of our user interfaces. This helped us determine buttons and features that would go on application to optimize its functionality. The purpose of paper prototyping is usability testing, where the user will go through a series of tasks using our prototype to see how efficient our design currently is.
The prototype featured 3 key tasks:

  • Find a van-accessible parking spot close to Denny Hall
  • Find an accessible route from Health Science building to Denny Hall
  • Find a map showing accessible features of Denny Hall

Phase 4: Refinement

Usability Testing & Wireframing

Using the paper prototypes, we evaluated the feedback and findings that we received from the usability test. By evaluating our results, we were able to see the problems and changes that were needed to make in our design to ensure clarity and efficiency.
From there, we analyzed the data, made changes, and created a detailed wireframe with annotations to the product.

Result

High-fidelity Mockup

After receiving feedback on the latest revision, I utilized that information to created the visual design of the app in Illustrator.

Future Directions

We plan to incorporate more features to make this app even more accessible and easy to use by our targeted users. Some examples of the features include:

  • Rating: Give user the ability to rate a route, a building, a parking lot. Data collected would be sent to UW's administratives to improve campus' accessibility.
  • Voice Command and Directions: Give user the option to keep their hands free. This is especially important for manual wheelchairs/walkers/crutches users.

Restroom Finder

Web Application Design and Development

  • Date: Autumn 2015
  • Skills: Full-stack Developing, Google App Engine, HTML, CSS, Bootstrap, Python
  • Category: Web Development

Overview

Project created for Interactive System Design and Technology (HCDE 310) - Autumn 2015
Restroom Finder is a complete web application built to help people find restrooms closest to an address they input.
Role: Full-stack developer, designer.

Problem

The problem I tried to address is something most of us have experienced at least once in our life when we’re out and about - the need to use a restroom, ASAP!
As annoying as this is, most of the time, it's viewed as an inconvenience. However, the need to have access to a restroom can be a critical factor for certain groups of people when they go out, for example:

  • People with medical issues that results in urgency, or the loss of bladder or bowel control - whether temporarily or permanently.
  • Parents with young children.
My goal is to design a system that would help lessen the anxiety and stress of planning for outings.

Process

The entire process from start to finish took 3 weeks.

Ideation

I know that I want to design something that would be helpful for people with different abilities - to help make their life easier in some aspects.
I started with many ideas, and narrowed down to 2:

  • Parking Finder
  • Restroom Finder
The main concepts of both applications were essentially the same: to return a relevant results displayed on a map using on location as the input.

Implementation

Since both ideas are similar in implementation details, I just needed to search for an API that would provide the data I needed, whethere it was parking or restroom information.
I started my search for APIs on Programmable Web. Also, one of my focuses was to make sure that the API would have an option to return accessible results to accommodate different needs, so I made sure to keep a look out for that as well.
Alas, I came across REFUGE Restroom API, which provided exactly what I need! And it also included a filter for accessibility! This shifted my focus to develop an application to find and display restrooms and narrow down my scope.

Workflow

Back-end Development

With Python as the back-end language, I utilized 2 APIs to process data - Google GeoCoding API, and REFUGE Restroom API.
I took the address that user entered, converted it to latitude and longitude data, and passed this into the REFUGE API to retrieve a list of restrooms close to that input in JSON form. I chose to take the top 10 results from the list.
With the result, I used Jinja2 to pass this information to the front-end to be displayed.

Front-end Development

I designed a simple 2-page website and implemented it with HTML5 and CSS3. I also made sure that it was responsive across platforms with Bootstrap. For accessibility, I included a toggle option that can be used to search for accessible restrooms.
After getting the information from the back-end, I used Google Maps Javascript API to display the map of the location, and markers to represent restrooms.

Result

The result is a simple two-page interactive website that let users enter an address, and return restrooms close by on a map.
